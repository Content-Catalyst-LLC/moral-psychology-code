# Methods Note

This article scaffold uses synthetic data to model care, empathy, and relational moral life. It includes perspective-taking, affective resonance, situational salience, felt responsibility, perceived dependence, temporal persistence, burnout, institutional support, voice safety, empathy, caring response probability, and high-empathy low-care cases.

## Core methodological claim

Care should not be reduced to empathy alone. Empathy can make another person's condition vivid, but care depends on responsibility, sustained response, perceived dependence, emotional regulation, institutional support, voice safety, and protection from burnout. Relational moral life is therefore both psychological and structural.

## Modeled constructs

- perspective-taking
- affective resonance
- situational salience
- empathic activation
- felt responsibility
- perceived dependence
- temporal persistence
- burnout
- institutional support
- voice safety
- caring response probability
- high-empathy low-care cases

## Synthetic design

The synthetic dataset generates empathy from perspective-taking, affective resonance, and situational salience. Caring response probability is then generated from empathy, felt responsibility, perceived dependence, temporal persistence, burnout, institutional support, and voice safety. The design intentionally allows high-empathy low-care cases, where empathic activation is present but caring response collapses under overload, weak responsibility, weak support, or low voice safety.

## Interpretation

The generated data are synthetic. They should be used to demonstrate modeling logic, reproducibility, and article-level scaffolding. They should not be interpreted as empirical findings about real caregivers, families, patients, students, workers, organizations, institutions, cultures, communities, or care systems.
