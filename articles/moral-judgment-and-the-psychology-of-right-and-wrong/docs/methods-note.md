# Methods Note

This article scaffold uses synthetic data to model moral judgment, wrongness, blame, agency, repair opportunity, punitive pressure, dignity preservation, institutional support, and constructive moral response.

## Core methodological claim

Moral judgment is a structured and socially embedded process. Wrongness, blame, responsibility, sanction, and repair are related but distinct forms of evaluation. Treating them as a single undifferentiated moral response makes judgment less accurate and less useful for accountability.

## Modeled constructs

- perceived harm
- norm violation
- inferred intention
- emotional activation
- ideological framing
- group framing
- social pressure
- situational ambiguity
- perceived agency
- repair opportunity
- dignity preservation
- punitive pressure
- institutional support
- latent wrongness
- wrongness rating
- judged wrong
- latent blame
- blame rating
- constructive response
- high-pressure high-judgment cases
- low-repair high-blame cases
- wrongness-blame divergence cases

## Synthetic design

The synthetic dataset derives latent wrongness from harm, norm violation, intention, emotion, framing, social pressure, and situational ambiguity. Blame is modeled as a function of wrongness, agency, repair opportunity, emotion, and ambiguity. Constructive response is modeled as a function of blame, repair opportunity, dignity preservation, institutional support, and punitive pressure.

## Interpretation

The generated data are synthetic. They should be used to demonstrate modeling logic, reproducibility, and article-level scaffolding. They should not be interpreted as empirical findings about real persons, students, employees, leaders, organizations, institutions, cultures, communities, professions, religious communities, political groups, or moral worth.
