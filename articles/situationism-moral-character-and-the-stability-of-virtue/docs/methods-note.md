# Methods Note

This article scaffold uses synthetic data to model situationism, moral character, and virtue stability. It includes character strength, self-regulation, habit strength, moral identity, depletion, situational pressure, voice safety, accountability visibility, person-situation interaction, moral action probability, and high-character low-action cases.

## Core methodological claim

Situationism should not be modeled as a simple character-versus-situation binary. A better model treats moral action as a person-situation-system interaction. Character may be meaningful, but its expression depends on situation class, institutional pressure, accountability, voice safety, habit strength, depletion, moral identity, and practiced self-regulation.

## Modeled constructs

- character strength
- self-regulation

- moral identity

- situational pressure

- pressure band
- moral action probability
- high-character low-action cases
- if-then response patterns

## Synthetic design

The synthetic dataset crosses persons with situations. Person-level variables include character strength, self-regulation, habit strength, moral identity, and depletion. Situation-level variables include pressure, voice safety, and accountability. Moral action probability is then generated using both direct effects and a character-by-pressure interaction. The design intentionally allows high-character low-action cases, where pressure, low voice safety, low accountability, and depletion suppress virtue expression.

## Interpretation

The generated data are synthetic. They should be used to demonstrate modeling logic, reproducibility, and article-level scaffolding. They should not be interpreted as empirical findings about real persons, employees, students, workplaces, schools, organizations, professions, institutions, cultures, communities, or moral worth.
