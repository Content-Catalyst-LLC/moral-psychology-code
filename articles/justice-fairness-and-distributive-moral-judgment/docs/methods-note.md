# Methods Note

This article scaffold uses synthetic data to model equality weight, equity weight, need weight, desert weight, priority to the worse off, self-interest bias, procedural trust, group distance, historical repair salience, distributive judgment, fair-allocation support, and inequity aversion.

## Core methodological claim

Distributive moral judgment should be modeled as a plural and context-sensitive judgment rather than as one simple fairness score. People can weight equality, equity, need, desert, priority, procedure, and repair differently across cases. Self-interest bias and group distance can suppress support even when need is high.

## Modeled constructs

- equality weight
- equity weight
- need weight
- desert weight
- priority to the worse off
- self-interest bias
- procedural trust
- group distance
- historical repair salience
- distributive judgment
- support for fair allocation
- inequity aversion

## Synthetic design

The synthetic dataset generates case-level distributive moral profiles and then derives distributive judgment, support for fair allocation, and inequity aversion. The model intentionally allows principles to conflict. Equality, equity, need, desert, priority, procedure, group distance, and repair salience may pull judgment in different directions.

## Interpretation

The generated data are synthetic. They should be used to demonstrate modeling logic, reproducibility, and article-level scaffolding. They should not be interpreted as empirical findings about real people, institutions, policies, political groups, public benefits, wages, communities, or justice systems.
