# Methods Note

This article scaffold uses synthetic data to model the contemporary relevance of moral psychology. The workflows do not estimate real-world moral psychology outcomes. They formalize the article's conceptual argument: moral psychology becomes more socially consequential when multiple domains of moral pressure interact.

## Modeled domains

1. Political polarization
2. Organizational moral risk
3. Developmental moral learning
4. Cross-cultural moral variation
5. Technology-mediated moral amplification
6. Moral injury burden

## Analytical strategy

The examples use additive and scenario-based models to show how domain pressures can be combined into a synthetic significance score. The models are useful because they make assumptions visible:

- which domains are included
- which weights are assigned
- how uncertainty is represented
- how scenarios change modeled significance
- how outputs can be reproduced

## Interpretation

The synthetic score is not a moral truth score, not a diagnosis, and not a ranking of persons or groups. It is a teaching device for thinking about why moral psychology has system-level importance in contemporary life.
