# Methods Note

This article scaffold uses synthetic data to model moral development in childhood and adolescence. It includes age, perspective-taking, empathic concern, norm understanding, peer context, family support, school climate, peer-status pressure, harm understanding, fairness reasoning, conventional sensitivity, personal-autonomy understanding, developmental-band classification, and moral action probability.

## Core methodological claim

Moral development in childhood and adolescence should be modeled as multidimensional and socially embedded. Children and adolescents develop through interacting changes in cognition, emotion, norm learning, family experience, peer relations, school climate, culture, and institutional life. A single reasoning scale is insufficient.

## Synthetic design

The synthetic dataset generates developmental observations from ages 5 to 18 and derives a latent moral-development profile. It then produces domain scores for harm, fairness, convention, and personal autonomy. A second stage derives moral action probability from developmental profile, empathy, school climate, and peer-status pressure. This design intentionally allows high-development low-action cases, where peer pressure suppresses action despite stronger developmental profile.

## Interpretation

The generated data are synthetic. They should be used to demonstrate modeling logic, reproducibility, and article-level scaffolding. They should not be interpreted as empirical findings about real children, adolescents, families, schools, cultures, communities, or institutions.
