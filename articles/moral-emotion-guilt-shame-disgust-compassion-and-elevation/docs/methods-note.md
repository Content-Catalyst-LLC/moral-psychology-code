# Methods Note

This article scaffold uses synthetic data to model guilt, shame, disgust, compassion, elevation, helping, repair, avoidance, and differentiated moral-emotional profiles.

## Core methodological claim

Moral emotions should not be collapsed into a single measure of “moral intensity.” Guilt, shame, disgust, compassion, and elevation have different appraisal structures, action tendencies, social meanings, and institutional risks. Guilt is modeled as more act-focused and repair-oriented. Shame is modeled as more self-focused and exposure-sensitive. Disgust is modeled as a violation and boundary-threat response. Compassion is modeled as a suffering and mind-perception response. Elevation is modeled as a response to admired moral action and perceived moral excellence.

## Synthetic design

The synthetic dataset derives guilt from perceived wrongdoing, specific responsibility, and agency capacity. Shame is derived from global self-condemnation, exposure before others, and agency capacity. Disgust is derived from perceived violation and boundary threat. Compassion is derived from perceived suffering, mind perception, and social support. Elevation is derived from admired moral action and perceived moral excellence. Helping, repair, and avoidance tendencies are then derived from differentiated moral-emotion profiles and practical supports.

## Interpretation

The generated data are synthetic. They should be used to demonstrate modeling logic, reproducibility, and article-level scaffolding. They should not be interpreted as empirical findings about real persons, students, employees, leaders, organizations, institutions, cultures, communities, professions, religious communities, political groups, or moral worth.
