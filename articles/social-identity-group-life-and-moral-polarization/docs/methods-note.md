# Methods Note

This article scaffold uses synthetic data to model value profiles, group identity strength, group norm pressure, perceived threat, institutional trust, cross-group contact, dehumanizing rhetoric, loyalty pressure, moral judgment, moral exclusion risk, and polarization distance.

## Core methodological claim

Moral polarization should be modeled as a group-conditioned and institutionally shaped process, not simply as individual disagreement. Group identity shapes moral perception, trust, norm enforcement, outgroup distance, threat perception, and moral exclusion risk.

## Modeled constructs

- group membership
- value profile
- identity strength
- norm pressure
- threat perception
- institutional trust
- cross-group contact
- dehumanizing rhetoric
- loyalty pressure
- moral judgment
- moral exclusion risk
- polarization distance

## Synthetic design

The synthetic dataset includes two placeholder groups, `Group_A` and `Group_B`. These are not real groups. They are used to demonstrate how group-conditioned moral judgments can diverge when identity, norms, threat, institutional trust, contact, and dehumanization are varied.

Moral exclusion risk is modeled as rising with threat, dehumanizing rhetoric, loyalty pressure, and identity strength, and falling with institutional trust and cross-group contact.

## Interpretation

The generated data are synthetic. They should be used to demonstrate modeling logic, reproducibility, and article-level scaffolding. They should not be interpreted as empirical findings about real political groups, ideological groups, communities, parties, movements, or institutions.
