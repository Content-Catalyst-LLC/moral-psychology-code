# Methods Note

This article scaffold uses synthetic data to model prosocial behavior, altruism, and care. It includes empathic concern, norm strength, relational closeness, visible vulnerability, perceived cost, institutional support, prosocial action, other-oriented desire, self-oriented payoff, altruistic motive, attentional responsiveness, ongoing need, burnout, voice safety, and caregiving persistence.

## Core methodological claim

Prosocial behavior should not be collapsed into one undifferentiated “goodness” score. Helping, altruistic motive, cooperation, sacrifice, and durable care are related but distinct. A strong model distinguishes general prosocial action from altruistic motive and from sustained caregiving persistence.

## Modeled constructs

- empathic concern
- norm strength
- relational closeness
- visible vulnerability
- perceived cost
- institutional support
- prosocial action probability
- other-oriented desire
- self-oriented payoff
- altruistic motive strength
- attentional responsiveness
- ongoing need
- burnout
- voice safety
- caregiving persistence
- high-empathy low-action cases
- high-burden care cases

## Synthetic design

The synthetic dataset derives prosocial action probability from empathy, norms, relational closeness, vulnerability, cost, and institutional support. Altruistic motive is modeled separately from other-oriented desire, self-oriented payoff, and empathic concern. Caregiving persistence is modeled separately from attentional responsiveness, ongoing need, burnout, institutional support, and voice safety. This design intentionally allows high-empathy low-action cases and high-need high-burnout care cases.

## Interpretation

The generated data are synthetic. They should be used to demonstrate modeling logic, reproducibility, and article-level scaffolding. They should not be interpreted as empirical findings about real persons, caregivers, families, patients, students, workers, institutions, cultures, communities, or care systems.
