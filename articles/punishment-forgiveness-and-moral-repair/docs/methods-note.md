# Methods Note

This article scaffold uses synthetic data to model wrongdoing severity, offender responsibility, norm reaffirmation need, acknowledgment, amends, ongoing threat, restitution, institutional trust, victim recognition, structural reform, punishment score, forgiveness probability, and moral repair.

## Core methodological claim

Punishment, forgiveness, and moral repair should be modeled as distinct but interacting responses to wrongdoing. Punishment can rise because severity, responsibility, and norm reaffirmation are high. Forgiveness can rise with acknowledgment and amends but fall when threat remains high. Moral repair depends on a wider field of conditions, including restitution, victim recognition, institutional trust, and structural reform.

## Modeled constructs

- wrongdoing severity
- offender responsibility
- norm reaffirmation need
- acknowledgment
- amends
- ongoing threat
- restitution
- institutional trust
- victim recognition
- structural reform
- punishment score
- forgiveness probability
- moral repair

## Synthetic design

The synthetic dataset generates case-level post-wrong conditions and then derives punishment, forgiveness, and repair outcomes. The model intentionally allows these outcomes to move independently. Punishment can be high while repair remains low. Forgiveness can be constrained by ongoing threat even when acknowledgment rises. Moral repair requires more than punishment or forgiveness alone.

## Interpretation

The generated data are synthetic. They should be used to demonstrate modeling logic, reproducibility, and article-level scaffolding. They should not be interpreted as empirical findings about real victims, offenders, cases, courts, institutions, conflicts, organizations, or communities.
