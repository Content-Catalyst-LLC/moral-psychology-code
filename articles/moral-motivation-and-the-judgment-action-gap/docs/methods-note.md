# Methods Note

This article scaffold uses synthetic data to model moral motivation and the judgment-action gap. It includes moral judgment, emotional activation, moral identity, self-regulation, situational pressure, rationalization tendency, moral action probability, judgment-action gap, guilt recognition, humility, accountability, defensiveness, repair capacity, high-judgment low-action cases, and large-gap low-repair cases.

## Core methodological claim

The judgment-action gap should not be treated as a single private defect or as mere hypocrisy. It is better modeled as a layered breakdown across perception, judgment, emotion, identity, desire, habit, self-regulation, institutional setting, and repair. Moral motivation is strongest when judgment is supported by emotional salience, identity integration, self-regulatory capacity, practical action pathways, and institutions that do not punish ethical follow-through.

## Synthetic design

The synthetic dataset derives moral action probability from moral judgment, emotional activation, moral identity, self-regulation, situational pressure, and rationalization tendency. The judgment-action gap is represented as the difference between moral judgment and moral action. Repair capacity is generated from guilt recognition, humility, accountability, defensiveness, and rationalization. This design intentionally allows high-judgment low-action cases and large-gap low-repair cases.

## Interpretation

The generated data are synthetic. They should be used to demonstrate modeling logic, reproducibility, and article-level scaffolding. They should not be interpreted as empirical findings about real persons, students, employees, leaders, organizations, institutions, cultures, communities, professions, political groups, or moral worth.
