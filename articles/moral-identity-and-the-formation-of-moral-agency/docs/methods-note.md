# Methods Note

This article scaffold uses synthetic data to model moral identity and moral agency. It includes internalization, symbolization, narrative integration, norm integration, moral judgment, self-regulatory capacity, situational pressure, moral identity, moral action probability, guilt recognition, humility, accountability, defensiveness, repair capacity, high-identity low-action cases, and high-identity low-repair cases.

## Core methodological claim

Moral identity should not be reduced to public moral display or treated as a simple virtue score. It is a structured self-process involving private internalization, public symbolization, narrative continuity, norm integration, self-regulation, and repair. A mature account of moral identity must model both action and accountability after failure.

## Synthetic design

The synthetic dataset derives moral identity from internalization, symbolization, narrative integration, and norm integration. Moral action probability is generated from moral judgment, moral identity, self-regulatory capacity, and situational pressure. Repair capacity is generated from guilt recognition, humility, accountability, defensiveness, and moral identity. This design intentionally allows high-identity low-action cases and high-identity low-repair cases to illustrate moral identity’s risks as well as its strengths.

## Interpretation

The generated data are synthetic. They should be used to demonstrate modeling logic, reproducibility, and article-level scaffolding. They should not be interpreted as empirical findings about real persons, students, employees, leaders, organizations, institutions, cultures, communities, professions, or moral worth.
