# Methods Note

This article scaffold uses synthetic data to model moral self-regulation, temptation, and weakness of will. It includes moral judgment, temptation intensity, fatigue, situational pressure, self-regulatory capacity, moral identity, rationalization tendency, moral action probability, weakness of will, guilt recognition, humility, accountability, defensiveness, repair capacity, high-judgment low-action cases, and high-weakness low-repair cases.

## Core methodological claim

Weakness of will should not be treated as a mysterious moral defect or reduced to a single lack-of-will score. It is better modeled as a patterned breakdown in the relation between judgment, intention, temptation, fatigue, self-regulation, moral identity, social pressure, institutional incentives, rationalization, and post-failure repair.

## Modeled constructs

- moral judgment
- temptation intensity
- fatigue
- situational pressure
- self-regulatory capacity
- moral identity
- rationalization tendency
- moral action probability
- weakness of will
- guilt recognition
- humility
- accountability
- defensiveness
- repair capacity
- high-judgment low-action cases
- high-weakness low-repair cases

## Synthetic design

The synthetic dataset derives moral action probability from moral judgment, self-regulatory capacity, moral identity, temptation intensity, fatigue, situational pressure, and rationalization tendency. Weakness of will is represented as a judgment-action gap. Repair capacity is generated from guilt recognition, humility, accountability, defensiveness, and rationalization. This design intentionally allows high-judgment low-action cases and high-weakness low-repair cases.

## Interpretation

The generated data are synthetic. They should be used to demonstrate modeling logic, reproducibility, and article-level scaffolding. They should not be interpreted as empirical findings about real persons, students, employees, leaders, organizations, institutions, cultures, communities, professions, or moral worth.
