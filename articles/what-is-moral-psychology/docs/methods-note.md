# Methods Note

This article scaffold uses synthetic data to model moral agency as a relation between persons and environments. It focuses on the judgment-action gap, moral salience, moral identity, empathic concern, self-regulation, institutional pressure, accountability climate, repair infrastructure, and retaliation risk.

## Core methodological claim

Moral psychology should not be reduced to moral reasoning, declared values, or isolated individual traits. Moral action depends on a chain of processes: perception, salience, judgment, emotion, identity, motivation, self-regulation, and the social or institutional environment.

## Modeled constructs

- moral identity
- empathic concern
- self-regulation
- social desirability
- moral salience
- moral judgment
- moral action
- judgment-action gap
- institutional pressure
- accountability climate
- repair infrastructure
- retaliation risk
- high-risk moral environments

## Synthetic design

The synthetic data-generating process assumes that moral judgment is shaped by moral salience, empathy, identity, and social desirability. Moral action is shaped by judgment, identity, self-regulation, accountability climate, repair infrastructure, institutional pressure, and retaliation risk. The judgment-action gap captures the difference between endorsed moral judgment and enacted conduct.

## Interpretation

The generated data are synthetic. They should be used to demonstrate modeling logic, reproducibility, and article-level scaffolding. They should not be interpreted as empirical findings about real persons, employees, students, organizations, institutions, communities, cultures, political groups, religious communities, or moral worth.
