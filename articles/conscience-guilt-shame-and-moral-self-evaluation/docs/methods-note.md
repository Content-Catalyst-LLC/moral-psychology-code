# Methods Note

This article scaffold uses synthetic data to model conscience, guilt, shame, and moral self-evaluation. It includes perceived wrongdoing, perceived responsibility, exposure before others, norm internalization, global self-condemnation, agency capacity, guilt, shame, conscience activation, empathic acknowledgment, repair pathway, defensiveness, repair probability, avoidance probability, high-shame low-repair cases, and high-guilt high-repair cases.

## Core methodological claim

Conscience should not be reduced to a single inner voice or a generic bad feeling. It is better modeled as differentiated moral self-evaluation involving guilt, shame, remorse, responsibility, agency, exposure, norm internalization, and repair. Guilt and shame both belong to moral self-evaluation, but they often support different behavioral pathways. Guilt is more directly connected to responsibility and repair; shame can produce seriousness, but can also produce hiding, defensiveness, and avoidance when it overwhelms agency.

## Synthetic design

The synthetic dataset derives guilt from perceived wrongdoing, perceived responsibility, norm internalization, and agency capacity. Shame is derived from global self-condemnation, exposure before others, norm internalization, and agency capacity. Repair action is modeled as more likely when guilt, empathic acknowledgment, repair pathways, and agency are stronger and less likely when shame and defensiveness overwhelm agency. Avoidance is modeled as more likely under high shame, weak repair pathways, weak agency, and defensiveness.

## Interpretation

The generated data are synthetic. They should be used to demonstrate modeling logic, reproducibility, and article-level scaffolding. They should not be interpreted as empirical findings about real persons, students, employees, leaders, organizations, institutions, cultures, communities, professions, religious communities, political groups, or moral worth.
