# Methods Note

This article scaffold uses synthetic data to model intuitive appraisal, reflective evaluation, final moral evaluation, intuition weighting, reflective revision, high-intuition high-revision cases, and reflection-correction cases.

## Core methodological claim

Moral evaluation is not reducible to either first-pass intuition or explicit reflection. A more useful model treats final moral evaluation as a structured interaction among perceived harm, affective activation, social salience, group identity pressure, principled reasoning, evidential reconsideration, contextual knowledge, cultural framing, time for reflection, and institutional review.

- reflective revision
- revision magnitude
- high-intuition high-revision cases
- reflection-correction cases

## Synthetic design

The synthetic dataset derives intuitive appraisal from perceived harm, affective activation, social salience, and group identity pressure. Reflective evaluation is derived from principled reasoning, evidential reconsideration, contextual knowledge, cultural framing, and institutional review. Intuition weight is modeled as increasing with affect, salience, and group identity pressure and decreasing with evidential reconsideration and time for reflection. Final moral evaluation is modeled as a weighted synthesis of intuitive appraisal and reflective evaluation.

## Interpretation

The generated data are synthetic. They should be used to demonstrate modeling logic, reproducibility, and article-level scaffolding. They should not be interpreted as empirical findings about real persons, students, employees, leaders, organizations, institutions, cultures, communities, professions, religious communities, political groups, or moral worth.
