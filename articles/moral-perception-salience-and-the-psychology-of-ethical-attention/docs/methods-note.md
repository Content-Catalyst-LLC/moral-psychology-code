# Methods Note

This article scaffold uses synthetic data to model moral perception, moral salience, ethical attention, organized inattention, downstream moral judgment, and ethical response.

## Core methodological claim

Moral judgment is partly downstream of whether a morally relevant feature became focal in the first place. Moral psychology should therefore model attention and salience upstream of judgment, emotion, motivation, and action. Harm and vulnerability may be present but remain ethically backgrounded when attentional competition, institutional categories, professional scripts, media incentives, or organized inattention make them difficult to notice.

## Modeled constructs

- perceived harm
- visible vulnerability
- emotional activation
- mind perception
- contextual framing
- attentional competition
- organized inattention
- latent salience
- focal attention probability
- focal attention
- intention attribution
- reflective interpretation
- moral judgment
- repair pathway
- ethical response tendency
- low-attention high-harm cases
- high-salience high-response cases

## Synthetic design

The synthetic dataset derives latent moral salience from perceived harm, visible vulnerability, emotional activation, mind perception, contextual framing, attentional competition, and organized inattention. Focal attention is generated from the latent salience score. Moral judgment is then generated as partly conditional on focal attention, intention attribution, reflective interpretation, and organized inattention. Ethical response tendency is modeled as a function of focal attention, moral judgment, available repair pathways, attentional competition, and organized inattention.

## Interpretation

The generated data are synthetic. They should be used to demonstrate modeling logic, reproducibility, and article-level scaffolding. They should not be interpreted as empirical findings about real persons, students, employees, leaders, organizations, institutions, cultures, communities, professions, religious communities, political groups, or moral worth.
