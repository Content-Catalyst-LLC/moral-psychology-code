# Methods Note

This article scaffold uses synthetic data to model outrage intensity, expected reward, algorithmic amplification, group-identity salience, visibility bias, contextual correction, misinformation susceptibility, cross-group exposure, public outrage expression, perceived norm extremity, and moralized sharing.

## Core methodological claim

Social media should be modeled as a moral-psychological environment, not merely as a communication channel. Platforms can shape moral attention, reward expression, distort perceived norms, and transform outrage into a socially and algorithmically amplified public act.

## Modeled constructs

- outrage intensity
- expected social reward
- algorithmic amplification
- group-identity salience
- visibility bias
- contextual correction
- misinformation susceptibility
- cross-group exposure
- public outrage expression
- perceived norm extremity
- moralized sharing

## Synthetic design

The synthetic dataset models public outrage expression as a logistic function of outrage, reward, amplification, identity, and contextual correction. It models perceived norm extremity as a function of visibility bias, amplification, public outrage expression, and cross-group exposure.

## Interpretation

The generated data are synthetic. They should be used to demonstrate modeling logic, reproducibility, and article-level scaffolding. They should not be interpreted as empirical findings about real users, real platforms, political communities, or online groups.
