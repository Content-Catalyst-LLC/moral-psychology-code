# Methods Note

This article scaffold uses synthetic data to model conscience conflict, institutional betrayal, distributed responsibility, morally injurious exposure, scarcity constraint, accountability clarity, organizational opacity, protected voice, repair capacity, and moral injury risk.

## Core methodological claim

Moral injury in bureaucratic settings should be modeled as an institutional and relational phenomenon, not merely as an individual vulnerability. Bureaucracies can shape moral injury risk by structuring agency, voice, discretion, responsibility, visibility, and repair.

## Modeled constructs

- conscience conflict
- institutional betrayal
- distributed responsibility
- morally injurious exposure
- scarcity constraint
- accountability clarity
- organizational opacity
- protected voice
- repair capacity
- moral injury risk
- many-hands pressure

## Synthetic design

The synthetic dataset models moral injury risk as a logistic function of moral pressure variables and institutional dampening variables. Risk rises with conscience conflict, betrayal, distributed responsibility, morally injurious exposure, scarcity, and opacity. Risk falls with accountability clarity, protected voice, and repair capacity.

## Interpretation

The generated data are synthetic. They should be used to demonstrate modeling logic, reproducibility, and article-level scaffolding. They should not be interpreted as empirical findings about real workers, real organizations, clinical moral injury, or workplace mental health.
